Reason 1: Overleveraging Early

01

The "Quick Start" Trap

The most common single cause of challenge failure. A trader starts their evaluation, wants to build a comfortable buffer quickly, and takes a position that's too large relative to their account. One bad trade — or one bad day — and they've violated either the daily loss limit or the maximum drawdown. Challenge over, fee lost, start again.

This happens because traders treat the early days of a challenge as a sprint. They want to get ahead of the targets quickly, so they size up. But FTMO's 5% daily loss limit is unforgiving. On a $100,000 account, that's $5,000. One oversized position in a volatile session and that limit is gone before lunch.

The correct approach is the opposite of what most traders instinctively do. Start conservative. Build slowly. Protect the drawdown at all costs. The profit targets are achievable with consistent small wins. They're not achievable with a blown daily limit on day three.

The maths most traders ignore: To recover from a 5% daily loss while staying within the 10% maximum drawdown, you have zero margin for another bad day. The challenge becomes nearly impossible from that position. Don't let it happen.

Reason 2: Emotional Decision-Making After Losses

02

Revenge Trading and Strategy Abandonment

The second killer. A trader has a losing day — entirely normal, entirely expected in any viable trading strategy. But because real money (their evaluation fee) is on the line, the loss feels different. They deviate from their strategy. They take trades they normally wouldn't. They increase position size to "make it back." This is revenge trading, and it ends challenges far more often than any single bad market day.

Every trader who has passed a prop firm evaluation will tell you the same thing: the rules aren't the hard part. The hard part is executing your strategy exactly as you would in a normal week, while also knowing that one mistake ends the entire evaluation.

That psychological pressure changes decision-making in ways traders consistently underestimate. Studies on performance under pressure consistently show that high-stakes environments cause people to revert to instinctive behaviour — which, for most traders, means overtrading and increasing risk exactly when they should be doing the opposite.

  • ✕Taking trades outside your strategy's criteria because you're behind target
  • ✕Increasing lot size after a loss to recover faster
  • ✕Moving stop losses to avoid being stopped out
  • ✕Trading during news events you'd normally avoid
  • ✕Cutting winners short while letting losers run during stressful sessions

Every item on that list is a symptom of the same problem: emotion overriding process. And every item on that list can end a challenge.

The key realisation: A prop firm evaluation doesn't test whether you have a good strategy. It tests whether you can execute your strategy consistently under pressure. Those are very different things — and most traders only discover the difference after their first failure.

Reason 3: Misunderstanding the Rules

03

Violated Rules That Were Never Understood Clearly

The third reason — and in some ways the most avoidable — is rule violations that come from misunderstanding the evaluation terms. FTMO's rules look simple on the surface, but they have nuances that catch traders off guard. News trading restrictions, weekend holding prohibitions, the exact calculation method for daily loss limits — these details matter enormously.

The Most Commonly Misunderstood FTMO Rules

  • ✕
    Daily loss calculation: Many traders think the 5% daily limit resets from current equity. It resets from account balance — meaning an open losing position counts against the limit in ways traders don't anticipate.
  • ✕
    News trading: Positions cannot be held open during major high-impact news. Traders who leave trades running through NFP or FOMC releases get automatically flagged.
  • ✕
    Weekend holding: All positions must be closed before the Friday close. This catches swing traders consistently.
  • ✕
    Minimum trading days: You cannot hit the profit target in 3 days. Even if your account is up 10% on day 3, you must continue trading until you've been in the market for at least 4 separate days.
  • ✕
    The consistency rule (FTMO specific): Some FTMO accounts carry a consistency rule requiring that no single day's profit exceeds 30% of total profits. One exceptional day can disqualify you even if you passed every other criterion.

None of these rules are hidden. They're all in FTMO's documentation. But many traders skim the terms, assume they understand the basics, and discover these nuances at the worst possible moment — when they've just violated one.
